How to Use the Retirement Calculator

Enter your current age and target retirement age to set your timeline. Input your current retirement savings (401k, IRA, investments combined), your planned monthly contribution, and your expected annual investment return. The calculator projects your balance at retirement and estimates monthly income using the 4% withdrawal rule.

Retirement Savings Formula

FV = PV × (1+r)ⁿ + PMT × [(1+r)ⁿ − 1] ÷ r

Where: FV = Future value at retirement, PV = Present savings, r = Monthly return rate, n = Months until retirement, PMT = Monthly contribution

Frequently Asked Questions

A common rule of thumb is the 25x rule: multiply your expected annual retirement expenses by 25. If you plan to spend $60,000/year in retirement, you'd need $1,500,000 saved. This is based on the 4% safe withdrawal rate, which has historically sustained a 30-year retirement through most market conditions.
The 4% rule states that you can safely withdraw 4% of your retirement portfolio in your first year of retirement, then adjust for inflation each year, without running out of money over a 30-year period. It was derived from the famous Trinity Study analyzing historical stock and bond returns from 1926 to 1995.
As early as possible. Due to compound interest, starting at 25 instead of 35 can result in more than double the retirement balance, even if you contribute the same amount each month. A 25-year-old saving $300/month at 7% will have about $900,000 by 65. A 35-year-old doing the same will have about $450,000.
At minimum, contribute enough to get your full employer match — that's essentially free money with an instant 50–100% return. Beyond that, financial advisors generally recommend saving 10–15% of your gross income for retirement, including employer contributions. In 2026, the 401k contribution limit is $23,500 (plus $7,500 catch-up if over 50).
For a diversified stock portfolio, historical average returns are about 10% nominal or 7% inflation-adjusted annually. For conservative planning, many advisors use 5–6% to account for fees and sequence-of-returns risk. Use 7% as a reasonable baseline. Always use lower rates as you approach retirement and shift to bonds.
