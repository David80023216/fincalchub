How to Pay Off Debt Faster

The most effective debt payoff strategies are the Debt Avalanche (pay highest interest first) and the Debt Snowball (pay smallest balance first). Mathematically, the avalanche saves the most money; the snowball provides psychological momentum.

Increasing your payment by even $50–$100/month can save hundreds or thousands in interest and months off your payoff date. Try different payment amounts in the calculator to see the impact.

Debt Payoff Formula

n = −log(1 − (P × r / M)) ÷ log(1 + r)

Where: n = Number of months to payoff, P = Balance, r = Monthly interest rate (APR ÷ 12), M = Monthly payment

Frequently Asked Questions

The fastest way is the Debt Avalanche method: list all debts by interest rate, make minimum payments on all, and put every extra dollar toward the highest-rate debt first. Once that's paid off, roll that payment to the next highest-rate debt. This minimizes total interest paid and gets you debt-free the fastest.
The Debt Snowball method focuses on paying off your smallest balance first, regardless of interest rate. While it costs more in interest than the avalanche method, it provides quick psychological wins that keep you motivated. Popularized by Dave Ramsey, it works well for people who need momentum to stay on track.
Even small extra payments have a big impact. On a $10,000 debt at 15% interest, a minimum payment of $200/month takes 79 months and costs $5,700 in interest. Paying $300/month takes only 40 months and costs $2,700 in interest — saving $3,000 and 3+ years!
If your debt interest rate is higher than your expected investment return, pay off debt first. High-interest debt (above 7–8%) should be paid off aggressively. For low-interest debt (below 4–5%), investing the difference may be mathematically better. Always prioritize getting any employer 401k match first — that's a guaranteed 50–100% return.
Minimum payments are designed to keep you in debt as long as possible, maximizing interest revenue for lenders. On a $5,000 credit card balance at 20% APR with 2% minimum payments, it takes over 30 years and costs more than $7,000 in interest to pay off. Always pay more than the minimum.
