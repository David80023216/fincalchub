BMI Categories & What They Mean

BMI Range Category Health Risk
Below 18.5 Underweight Increased risk
18.5 – 24.9 Normal Weight Lowest risk
25.0 – 29.9 Overweight Increased risk
30.0 and above Obese High risk

Note: BMI is a screening tool, not a diagnostic tool. It doesn't account for muscle mass, bone density, age, or distribution of fat. Consult a healthcare professional for a complete health assessment.

BMI Formula

BMI (metric) = weight (kg) ÷ height² (m²)   |   BMI (imperial) = 703 × weight (lbs) ÷ height² (in²)

Example: A person who is 5'10" (70 inches) and weighs 160 lbs: BMI = 703 × 160 ÷ (70²) = 703 × 160 ÷ 4900 ≈ 22.9 (Normal Weight)

Frequently Asked Questions

BMI is a useful population-level screening tool but has significant limitations for individuals. It doesn't distinguish between muscle and fat mass — a muscular athlete may have a "overweight" BMI while being very healthy. It also doesn't account for age, sex, ethnicity, or where body fat is distributed. For a complete health picture, consult your doctor.
For adults 20 and older, the CDC defines a healthy BMI as 18.5–24.9. However, the optimal range can vary by age, sex, and ethnicity. Some research suggests that for older adults (65+), a BMI of 22–27 may be associated with better outcomes. Asian populations may have increased health risks at lower BMI thresholds.
To lower BMI (reduce body fat), focus on: creating a moderate calorie deficit through diet and exercise, regular aerobic exercise (150+ min/week), strength training to preserve muscle, adequate sleep (7–9 hours), and reducing processed foods. Gradual, sustainable changes of 0.5–1 lb per week are more effective than crash diets.
BMI is calculated differently for children and teens (ages 2–19). For children, BMI is plotted on age- and sex-specific growth charts to get a BMI-for-age percentile. Below the 5th percentile is underweight; 5th–84th is healthy weight; 85th–94th is overweight; 95th percentile or above is obese. This calculator is designed for adults only.
Overweight (BMI 25–29.9) means a person has excess body weight for their height, which may increase health risks. Obese (BMI 30+) indicates a higher amount of excess body fat that significantly increases risk for conditions like type 2 diabetes, heart disease, hypertension, sleep apnea, and certain cancers. Obesity is further classified as Class I (30–34.9), Class II (35–39.9), and Class III (40+).
