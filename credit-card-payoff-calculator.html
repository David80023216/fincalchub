Understanding Credit Card Interest

Credit cards typically carry the highest interest rates of any consumer debt — commonly 20–30% APR. The average American credit card APR as of 2026 is around 22–24%. At these rates, carrying a balance is extremely costly.

Credit card interest compounds daily and is charged on your average daily balance each billing cycle. Even small balances can become significantly larger over time if only minimum payments are made.

How Credit Card Interest is Calculated

Daily Rate = APR ÷ 365  |  Monthly Interest = Balance × (APR ÷ 12)

For a $5,000 balance at 24.99% APR: monthly interest = $5,000 × (0.2499/12) = $104.13/month in interest charges alone. A $200 payment puts only $95.87 toward principal.

Frequently Asked Questions

It depends on your balance, interest rate, and monthly payment. A $5,000 balance at 22% APR with $150/month takes about 49 months (over 4 years) and $2,300 in interest. With $250/month, it takes just 26 months and $1,300 in interest. Use our calculator to see your specific scenario.
Credit card APRs in 2026 typically range from 15% (for excellent credit) to 30%+ (for poor credit). The average is around 22–24%. Any APR below 15% is considered good. If possible, look for 0% intro APR balance transfer cards to temporarily eliminate interest while paying down debt.
A 0% APR balance transfer card can be an excellent strategy if you can pay off the balance during the promotional period (usually 12–21 months). Watch out for transfer fees (typically 3–5% of the balance) and make sure you can pay it off before the promotional rate expires, as rates after the promo period are often higher.
Minimum payments are typically 1–2% of your balance or $25, whichever is greater. Paying only minimums on a $5,000 balance at 22% APR would take over 30 years to pay off and cost more than $8,000 in interest. Credit card statements are now required to show this calculation, which is why they include a "minimum payment warning."
Options include: (1) Call your card issuer and negotiate a lower rate — it works more often than you'd think; (2) Transfer to a 0% intro APR card; (3) Take out a personal loan at a lower rate to pay off the card; (4) Improve your credit score over time to qualify for lower rates. Building good payment history is the most reliable long-term strategy.
