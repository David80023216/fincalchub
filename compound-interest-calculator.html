How Compound Interest Works

Compound interest is often called the "eighth wonder of the world" (attributed to Albert Einstein). Unlike simple interest, which only earns interest on your original principal, compound interest earns interest on your interest — creating exponential growth over time.

The key factors are: principal amount, interest rate, time, and compounding frequency. Of these, time is the most powerful — starting early makes an enormous difference.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)

Where: A = Final amount, P = Principal, r = Annual interest rate (decimal), n = Compounding periods per year, t = Time in years

Example: $10,000 at 7% compounded monthly for 20 years: A = 10000 × (1 + 0.07/12)^(12×20) ≈ $40,064

Frequently Asked Questions

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Unlike simple interest, compound interest grows exponentially — your interest earns interest, creating a powerful snowball effect over time. This is why starting to invest early is so important.
The more frequently interest compounds, the more you earn. Daily compounding yields slightly more than monthly, which yields more than annually. The difference between daily and monthly compounding is small (fractions of a percent), but over decades it can add up to a meaningful amount.
The Rule of 72 is a quick mental math trick to estimate how long it takes to double your money. Simply divide 72 by your annual interest rate. At 6% annual return, your money doubles in approximately 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 9%, it doubles in about 8 years.
Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus all previously earned interest. Over 30 years at 7%, $10,000 grows to about $17,000 with simple interest but over $76,000 with compound interest — that's the power of compounding.
The S&P 500 has historically averaged about 10% annually (7% inflation-adjusted). High-yield savings accounts currently offer 4–5%. CDs may offer 4–6%. Bond funds typically return 3–5%. Your actual rate depends on your investment type, risk tolerance, and market conditions.
